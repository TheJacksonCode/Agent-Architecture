Section 02

Debate Flow

The structured argumentation process, from parallel research inputs through conflict identification to consensus formation.

Phase 1 -- Research Input
4 Parallel Research Agents Deliver Reports
Four research agents working in parallel produce HTML and Markdown reports covering ATS compatibility, UI/UX trends, recruitment best practices, and technical feasibility. These reports become the shared knowledge base for the debate.
Phase 2a -- Position Statements
🔬 Dr. Elena Voss
"ATS parseability is non-negotiable. Most companies use automated screening -- if the CV doesn't parse, it never reaches human eyes. Monochrome, single-column, clean typography."
🎨 Karol "Pixel"
"A traditional PDF doesn't showcase coding ability. An interactive web CV demonstrates real skills, is memorable, and differentiates from mass-produced CVs."
💼 Raven Blackwood
"Every bullet point must contain a quantifiable metric. Recruiters scan for numbers. 'Show don't tell' -- evidence-based claims only."
Phase 2b -- Conflict Surfaced
Elena vs. Karol -- Irreconcilable Positions
The primary tension is identified: ATS parsers need minimalism (Elena), while portfolio showcasing needs visual richness (Karol). These positions appear mutually exclusive within a single artifact.
Phase 2c -- Devil's Advocate Challenges
👁 Cien (Shadow)
"Do we really need Next.js? Doesn't NaukaAI look like a hobby? Isn't a dual solution over-engineering?"
Cien targets three risk areas: technical complexity, recruiter perception, and scope creep. Each challenge forces the group to justify and simplify their proposals.
Phase 2d -- Breakthrough Resolution
💼 Raven Blackwood -- Breakthrough
"DWA ARTEFAKTY z JEDNEGO ZRODLA" -- Two Artifacts from One Source
Rather than choosing between ATS PDF and interactive web CV, produce BOTH from a single TypeScript data source (cv-data.ts). This satisfies both Elena's and Karol's requirements while eliminating inconsistency risk.
Phase 2e -- Consensus & Output
FIVE_MINDS_REPORT.md Produced
The Gold Solution document is finalized with: executive summary, dual-output strategy, expert positions with attribution, 4 identified risks with mitigations, and implementation guidelines for Phase 3's three parallel coders.

Section 08

Recommendations

10 concrete improvements across structural, process, and integration dimensions.

Structural
R-01
Multi-Round Debate with Explicit Rebuttal Phase
Add 5 structured rounds: Position Statements, Cross-Examination, Synthesis Proposals, Devil's Advocate Stress Test, and Final Positions. Current single-pass debate may miss important counter-arguments.
Round 1: Position Statements (each expert presents) Round 2: Cross-Examination (experts challenge each other) Round 3: Synthesis Proposals (anyone can propose resolution) Round 4: Devil's Advocate Stress Test (Cien attacks proposals) Round 5: Final Positions and Consensus Vote
Structural
R-02
Formal Evaluation Rubric for Proposals
Introduce weighted scoring criteria: Feasibility (0.25), Risk Level (0.20), Domain Coverage (0.20), Simplicity (0.15), Stakeholder Satisfaction (0.20). Aligns with the existing Critique Agent rubric pattern.
Structural
R-03
Explicit 5th Mind Definition
Either rename to "Four Minds Protocol," formally designate the Orchestrator as the 5th Mind (Moderator/Synthesizer), or add a 5th expert such as an "End User Representative."
Structural
R-04
Structured Output Schema for Devil's Advocate
Ensure Cien's contributions are never lost by requiring structured JSON output: target_decision, challenge_type, question, worst_case_scenario, severity, resolution, and simplification_applied.
Structural
R-05
Dissent Preservation
Every overruled position should be recorded with: the dissenting position, why it was overruled, and under what conditions it should be reconsidered. Mirrors Anthropic's red-teaming philosophy.
Process
R-06
Pre-Debate Briefing Document
Before debate begins, each expert receives: all research outputs, a structured brief with specific questions to decide, constraints (budget, timeline, technical limitations), and evaluation criteria.
Process
R-07
Post-Debate Validation Loop
After the Five Minds report is produced, present the decision to research agents for verification. Any contradiction between research findings and the decision triggers a mini-debate round.
Process
R-08
Confidence-Weighted Consensus
Adopt from A-HMAD (2025): each expert assigns a confidence score (0-1) to the final decision. If any expert's confidence falls below 0.6, their concerns are escalated for explicit resolution.
Integration
R-09
Devil's Advocate as Standing Role Across All Phases
Extend Cien beyond Phase 2 into Phase 3 (Coding: "Is this implementation too complex?"), Phase 4 (Integration: "Are we coupling unnecessarily?"), and Phase 5 (QA: "Are our tests testing the right things?").
Integration
R-10
Debate Transcript as Context for QA
Make the full debate transcript (not just the report) available to QA agents, allowing them to automatically derive validation criteria from expert requirements instead of manual linkage.

Overall Strategic Assessment
The Five Minds Protocol is a genuinely novel contribution to the multi-agent design decision space. It occupies a unique niche that existing frameworks do not adequately address: structured expert deliberation for open-ended architectural decisions with built-in skepticism.
Strength
Produced a qualitatively superior outcome (dual-output) that no single expert or simple voting system would have generated.
Strength
Devil's Advocate role prevented groupthink and scope creep -- real, measurable value.
Strength
Mandatory decision gate between research and implementation is architecturally sound and universally adoptable.
Weakness
Single-pass debate limits the depth of argumentation. No multi-round exchange.
Weakness
Documentation gaps (missing Cien, naming inconsistency) undermine reproducibility.
Weakness
No formal evaluation criteria means the next use might not reach the same quality of outcome.
